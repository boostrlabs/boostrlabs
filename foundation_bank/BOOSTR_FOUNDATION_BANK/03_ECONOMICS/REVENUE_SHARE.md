# Revenue Share Model

## Core rule

The Revenue Share of BOOSTR Labs never means ownership of the partner's entire business, entire income, entire career, assets, royalties, or outside operations.

Revenue Share only applies to the specific modules, offers, products, services, campaigns, payment flows, or systems expressly defined in a signed Statement of Work, Revenue Share Addendum, or related agreement.

## Exclusions by default

Unless explicitly included in writing, Revenue Share excludes:

- Music royalties.
- Distribution revenue from ONErpm, DistroKid, etc.
- Pre-existing revenue.
- Clients not managed or generated through BOOSTR.
- Sales outside BOOSTR infrastructure.
- External contracts.
- Car deal profit not specifically routed through BOOSTR.
- Any business income not expressly defined in the agreement.

## Acceptable revenue share bases

Revenue share can be calculated from:

- Gross revenue from a defined BOOSTR offer.
- Net revenue after payment processing fees.
- Profit after agreed direct costs.
- Campaign-attributed sales.
- BOOSTR Payment Link transactions.
- Specific product/service collections.

The method must be defined in writing.

## Duration

Revenue share duration depends on module/SOW.

Possible durations:

- While module remains active.
- 3 months.
- 6 months.
- 12 months.
- Until partner switches to monthly fee.
- Until a maximum revenue share cap is reached.

## Why this matters

Revenue-sharing agreements are commonly used where revenue is uncertain and parties want aligned incentives. They must define percentage, revenue base, payment frequency, term, exclusions, and default/termination events clearly. BOOSTR should treat each revenue share as a defined commercial module, not a vague promise.

## BOOSTR-specific use cases

- Artist sells beats through BOOSTR Smart Link.
- Creator sells merch through BOOSTR Store.
- Barbero receives bookings/deposits through BOOSTR.
- Beauty business sells appointment packages.
- Campaign leads to paid purchases.
- BOOSTR creates a new product/offer for a partner and shares revenue from that offer.

## Rule against bypass

If BOOSTR creates, structures, markets, or processes an offer, the partner cannot intentionally bypass BOOSTR to avoid agreed fees. Anti-bypass terms should be part of the agreement.
